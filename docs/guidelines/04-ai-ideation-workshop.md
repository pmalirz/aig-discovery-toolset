# Guideline 04: AI Ideation Workshop

> **Duration:** 90 minutes (in-person) or 60 minutes (virtual) + async submission window
> **Participants:** Open to anyone in the organization — team leads, individual contributors, managers
> **Outputs:** Multiple filled `ai-use-idea.md` templates
> **Prerequisite:** Department Discovery sessions completed (or in progress)

---

## Purpose

The AI Ideation Workshop is the most democratic part of the assessment. It gives everyone — not just managers or technologists — a chance to propose where AI could help. The best AI ideas often come from the people closest to the work, who see inefficiencies that leadership never hears about.

This workshop must be **safe, fun, and inclusive**. Many participants will not be technical. Some will be anxious about AI. Your job is to create an environment where "I wish a computer could read these PDFs for me" is a valid and valued contribution.

---

## Workshop Formats

Choose the format that best fits the organization:

### Format A: In-Person Sticky Note Session (90 minutes)

**Best for:** Teams that are co-located, moderate group sizes (8–20 people)

Groups brainstorm on sticky notes, then cluster and refine ideas into templates.

### Format B: Virtual Workshop (60 minutes)

**Best for:** Remote or distributed teams, larger groups

Use a digital collaboration tool (Miro, Mural, or even a shared document) for brainstorming, then follow up with async template completion.

### Format C: Async Submission Window (1 week)

**Best for:** Organizations where scheduling a group workshop is impractical, or as a complement to Format A/B

Distribute the `ai-use-idea.md` template with plain-language instructions. Set a deadline. Offer optional 15-minute "idea coaching" calls for anyone who wants help filling it out.

### Recommended Approach

**Format A or B + Format C.** Run a live workshop to generate energy and initial ideas, then keep the submission window open for a week so people who think of ideas later (or who were too shy to speak up in the group) can still contribute.

---

## Workshop Facilitation (Format A — In-Person)

### Room Setup

- Tables arranged in small groups (4–6 people per table)
- Sticky notes and markers on each table
- A large wall or whiteboard for clustering ideas
- Printed copies of the `ai-use-idea.md` template (simplified version — see below)
- Refreshments — this should feel informal and creative, not like a meeting

### Agenda

| Time | Activity | Details |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:10 | **Welcome & framing** | Set the tone, explain the process, establish safety |
| 0:10–0:20 | **Ice-breaker: "AI in Daily Life"** | Warm up the room with a fun, non-threatening AI exercise |
| 0:20–0:25 | **Brainstorming prompts** | Present prompts on screen, give people a moment to think |
| 0:25–0:45 | **Individual brainstorming** | Everyone writes ideas on sticky notes (1 idea per note) |
| 0:45–1:00 | **Table clustering** | Small groups share and cluster similar ideas |
| 1:00–1:15 | **Group share-out** | Each table presents their top 2–3 ideas to the room |
| 1:15–1:25 | **Dot voting** | Everyone gets 3 dots to vote for the ideas they think have the most potential |
| 1:25–1:30 | **Wrap-up & next steps** | Explain how ideas will be evaluated, encourage async submissions |

---

### Welcome & Framing (10 minutes)

> "Welcome! Today is about one thing: your ideas. You know your work better than anyone — the tasks that take too long, the information that's hard to find, the decisions you wish you had better data for. We want to hear about those."

**Key messages:**

1. **Every idea is valid.** "There are no bad ideas in this session. 'I wish someone could sort my emails' is just as valuable as 'We should build a predictive model for customer churn.'"

2. **You don't need to be technical.** "You don't need to know how AI works. Just tell us the problem. We'll figure out whether AI can help."

3. **This is not about replacing jobs.** "We're looking for the boring, repetitive, time-consuming parts of your work — the stuff that makes you think 'there has to be a better way.' We want to free up your time for the work that matters."

4. **All ideas will be evaluated fairly.** "Every idea submitted goes through the same scoring framework. The best ideas get resourced. Ideas that aren't ready yet get documented and revisited when conditions change."

---

### Ice-Breaker: "AI in Daily Life" (10 minutes)

**Purpose:** Normalize AI by showing that people already use it without realizing it.

**Exercise:**

> "Before we start, let's see how many of you already use AI without thinking about it. Raise your hand if you've ever..."

- Used a spelling or grammar checker? 🙋 (That's AI.)
- Asked Siri, Alexa, or Google Assistant a question? 🙋 (That's AI.)
- Had your email app suggest a reply? 🙋 (That's AI.)
- Used Google Maps to avoid traffic? 🙋 (That's AI — it's predicting future traffic.)
- Had Netflix or Spotify recommend something you actually liked? 🙋 (That's AI.)

> "So you're all already AI users. Today we're asking: where else in your work could AI help like that?"

**Alternative ice-breaker for more technical groups:**

> "In pairs, take 2 minutes. Tell your partner about the most tedious task you did last week. Then your partner tries to explain it back to you as if briefing a very capable but very literal robot."

This is fun and also surfaces concrete task descriptions.

---

### Brainstorming Prompts (5 minutes)

Display these on screen. Give people 1 minute of silent thinking before writing.

#### Prompt Set 1: Finding the Pain

> 🔍 "What task do you do over and over — the same steps, different inputs — that feels like a robot could do it?"

> 📄 "Where do you spend time searching for information that should be easy to find?"

> 🧠 "What decisions do you make based on gut feeling because the data is too hard to analyze?"

#### Prompt Set 2: Imagining the Future

> ✨ "If you had a tireless, accurate assistant who could read any document and answer questions about it — what would you ask it?"

> ⏰ "If AI could give you back 5 hours a week, what would you do with that time?"

> 🎯 "What's something your team should be doing but can't, because routine work takes all your time?"

#### Prompt Set 3: Spotting Opportunities

> 📊 "Where does your team produce data or reports that nobody reads — or that arrive too late to be useful?"

> 🔄 "What process involves copying data from one system to another?"

> 🚨 "Where do mistakes happen most often? What would happen if those mistakes could be caught automatically?"

---

### Individual Brainstorming (20 minutes)

**Instructions:**

> "Write one idea per sticky note. Don't overthink it. If you have 10 ideas, write 10 notes. We'll filter and prioritize together."

**Format for each sticky note:**

```
PROBLEM: [What's the pain?]
AI COULD: [What would AI do?]
IMPACT: [What would improve?]
```

**Facilitator's role during this time:**
- Walk around, offer encouragement
- Help people who are stuck: "What took the most time last week? Could you imagine a computer helping with any part of that?"
- Capture ideas from people who prefer to talk rather than write — write the sticky note for them
- Remind people: "Even small ideas count. 'AI could sort our incoming emails by topic' is a real idea."

---

### Table Clustering (15 minutes)

Each table:
1. Everyone shares their sticky notes
2. Group similar ideas together (e.g., "document reading" ideas, "data entry" ideas, "reporting" ideas)
3. For each cluster, write a one-line summary on a larger card
4. Choose the 2–3 strongest ideas to present to the room

---

### Group Share-Out (15 minutes)

Each table presents their top 2–3 ideas. The consultant captures these on a shared board.

**Facilitator tips:**
- After each presentation, ask: "Does anyone from another table have a similar idea?" — this validates and strengthens ideas
- Note which ideas generate the most head-nodding and energy
- If an idea is vague, ask one clarifying question (but don't deep-dive — that happens later)

---

### Dot Voting (10 minutes)

Give everyone 3 dot stickers (or use digital polling).

> "Vote for the ideas you think would make the biggest difference to your work — not what's most technically cool, but what would actually help."

The dot voting results inform (but don't determine) prioritization. They're a signal of organizational energy and buy-in.

---

### Wrap-Up (5 minutes)

> "Thank you for your ideas. Here's what happens next:
> 1. I'll take all these ideas and help convert the strongest ones into our standard evaluation template
> 2. The submission window stays open until [date] — if you think of anything else, email [contact] or fill out the template and save it to [location]
> 3. Every idea will be scored using the same framework. You'll see the results."

---

## Converting Sticky Notes to `ai-use-idea.md`

After the workshop, the consultant converts the top ideas into proper `ai-use-idea.md` templates.

### For Each High-Potential Idea

1. Write up the idea in the `ai-use-idea.md` template
2. Fill in as much of the YAML frontmatter as you can from the workshop discussion
3. Mark fields you couldn't fill as needing follow-up
4. Set `status: submitted`
5. Save to `tracker/pillars/<pillar>/ideas/<idea-name>.md`

### For Ideas That Are Too Vague

- Contact the submitter for a 15-minute follow-up call
- Or pair them with the `aig-interview` skill (`--type ideation`) to fill in the gaps

### For Ideas That Are Clearly Out of Scope

- Don't discard them. Save them with `status: deferred` and a note explaining why they're out of scope
- Include them in the final report as "future consideration" items

## Simplified Template for Workshop Distribution

For non-technical participants, distribute this simplified version instead of the full `ai-use-idea.md`:

```markdown
# AI Idea Submission

**Your name:** 
**Your team:** 
**Date:** 

## What's the problem?
_Describe what takes too long, is too error-prone, or is too frustrating in your work._

## How could AI help?
_You don't need to be technical. "A computer that can read documents and fill in forms" is a perfect answer._

## What would improve?
_How would you measure success? Hours saved? Fewer errors? Faster turnaround? Happier customers?_

## What data is involved?
_What information would the AI need to work with? (emails, documents, database records, spreadsheets, etc.)_

## Anything else?
_Any concerns, dependencies, or ideas about how this could work?_
```

The consultant converts these into full `ai-use-idea.md` templates after collection.

## Tips for Inclusive Facilitation

### Making Non-Technical People Comfortable

- Use everyday language: "smart automation" instead of "machine learning model"
- Give examples from their domain: "In Claims, this might mean AI reading claim forms and filling in SAP automatically"
- Normalize uncertainty: "Nobody knows exactly how AI will work for your situation yet — that's what we're here to figure out together"
- Avoid jargon entirely: no "LLM", "RAG", "fine-tuning" — save that for the technical write-up

### Ensuring All Voices Are Heard

- Use written brainstorming (sticky notes) before verbal sharing — this gives introverts time to think
- Explicitly invite quiet participants: "Has anyone at this table not shared yet? I'd love to hear your perspective"
- Offer the async submission option prominently — some people's best ideas come after the meeting
- If seniority dynamics are visible (junior people deferring to managers), consider splitting into role-based tables

### Handling Skepticism

| Statement | Response |
|---|---|
| "We tried automation before and it failed" | "That's valuable experience. What failed about it? Understanding that helps us avoid the same mistakes." |
| "AI can't understand our complex work" | "You're right that the complex judgment part needs humans. But what about the prep work, the data entry, the searching?" |
| "This is just another management initiative that will go nowhere" | "I understand the skepticism. That's why every idea gets scored transparently. If the numbers don't support it, we say so." |
